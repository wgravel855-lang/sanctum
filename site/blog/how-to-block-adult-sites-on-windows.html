How to block adult sites on Windows (free, private, no spyware)

A practical guide from the makers of Sanctum

If you have decided you want adult content off your computer, the hard part is not the decision - it is making it stick across every browser, including the moment you most want to undo it. Here are the real options on Windows, honestly, with their trade-offs.

1. The Windows HOSTS file

The HOSTS file lets you point specific domains at a dead address so they never load. It is free and built in. But it only matches exact domains you list by hand, it does not cover subdomains or the endless list of sites, and anyone can open it in Notepad and delete a line in ten seconds. It is a start, not a solution.

2. DNS filtering

Every site your browser visits starts with a DNS lookup. Point your computer at a filtering DNS resolver and blocked lookups fail before a connection is ever made - and because this happens below the browser, it covers Chrome, Edge, Firefox, and their private windows at once. The catch: system DNS settings are easy to switch back, and some browsers quietly use their own encrypted DNS (DoH) that skips your settings entirely unless it is contained.

3. Your router

Filtering at the router covers every device on your home network. But it does nothing the moment you are on mobile data or another Wi-Fi network, laptops travel, and router settings are one login away from being turned off.

4. Browser extensions

Extensions are easy to install but easy to remove, they only protect the browser they are installed in, and most do nothing in incognito or a different browser. Convenient, but thin.

5. Dedicated blocking apps

Purpose-built apps do the most: system-wide filtering, a background service that keeps working, and a way to lock the settings so you cannot casually undo them. The things to watch for are cost, and whether the app is honest about what it does - some accountability tools quietly monitor and report your activity, which is a privacy trade you should make on purpose, not by accident.

What actually makes a blocker hold

Cutting through all of it, a blocker that works has four properties:

  • It covers every browser, including private windows, because it filters below the browser.
  • It keeps working in the background, so closing an app or killing a process does not switch it off.
  • It can outlast a craving. The barrier only has to be harder to remove than the urge is to wait out - urges pass in minutes.
  • It is honest and private. It should tell you plainly how it can be removed, and it should not send your browsing anywhere.

Sanctum is a free, open-source Windows app built around exactly those four properties. It filters every browser at the DNS and HOSTS layers, runs as a background service with a watchdog, and can lock its own settings for a chosen stretch so a 2am impulse cannot undo a clear-headed decision. It collects nothing - no account, no telemetry - and because it is open source you can verify that yourself.
