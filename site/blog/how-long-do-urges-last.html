How long do porn urges actually last?

From the makers of Sanctum

When you are in the middle of a craving it feels like it will last forever, or at least until you give in. That feeling is the most convincing lie an urge tells. The truth is more useful: urges are short.

Most cravings crest and start to fade within a few minutes. The really sharp part - the one that feels unbearable - is usually the briefest. Researchers who study addictive behaviour describe an urge as a wave: it builds, peaks, and comes back down on its own, whether or not you act on it. Left alone, a single wave often passes inside 10 to 20 minutes, and the peak is much shorter than that.

The catch is what happens if you keep feeding it. Every time you open a tab, glance at a thumbnail, or "just check," you pour a little more water into the wave. That is why the urge can seem to last for an hour - not because a craving naturally lasts an hour, but because you have been topping it up the whole time.

Urge surfing: don't fight it, outlast it

Psychologists have a name for the skill of riding a craving out without acting on it: urge surfing. The idea is deceptively simple. Instead of white-knuckling against the urge (which tends to make it louder) or giving in (which teaches your brain that urges must be obeyed), you do a third thing - you notice it, and you let it move through you.

In practice, for the next few minutes:

  • Name it. "This is a craving. It will peak and pass." Naming it turns down the volume.
  • Breathe slowly. A few long exhales tell your nervous system it is not an emergency. The urge is not a command.
  • Change your physical context. Stand up, get water, step outside, put your phone in another room. Motion breaks the loop.
  • Watch the clock, gently. Give it ten minutes before you decide anything. Almost every time, there is nothing left to decide.

None of this requires heroic willpower. It requires a short delay - and the wave does the rest.

Every wave you ride weakens the next one

Here is the part worth holding onto: each time you let an urge pass without acting on it, you are not just surviving a moment. You are teaching your brain that the craving is not a siren you have to obey. Do that a few times and the urges do not vanish overnight, but they get quieter, shorter, and easier to sit with. You are, slowly, rewiring the reflex.
